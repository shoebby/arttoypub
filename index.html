Introducing The CLICKY! Mini-Mag

Hi there, thank you for picking up this tiny little publication! Curious to hear what this is all about? Keep reading!

CLICKY! was made to highlight the digital art-making tools that go underdiscussed and underconsidered; the tools that dare to be toy, that deign to be visible after compile, that allow themselves to be non-deterministic and awkward.

As digital makers we're used to tools behaving like they're supposed to, adhering to industry standards and fitting neatly into a pipeline. This mag asks of you to consider stepping out of your Adobe comfort zone and into the wacky and weird wide world of art toys, a place where people make tools of alternative methods.

What if you composed music with polygons or code, instead of a piano roll? Or drew with ASCII characters instead of a rectangular, pressure-sensitive brush? Or made a game in an engine that is thoroughly uncompromising in what it produces, perhaps even harshly limiting?

I think these are good things to ask yourself and to try out, in the end it stands to benefit you as an artist to keep your mind open. Formulated one of these questions for yourself but can't find a tool to match it? Make it! Share it! Preferably with me as well!!!

First though, a few pointers:

RATINGS ARE PLAY - There is no objective way to grade a tool so I am not going to attempt to do so. Instead, ratings will be abstract and subjective, like many things in our lives are.

PAY NOTHING - This publication is free. I feel weird about receiving money for writing about other people's hard work so you should give them your money instead.

THE BLACKLIST - Some are barred from reading this publication, who upon reading will be hunted down for sport. This list includes the Trump administration, Jonathan Blow, rechts-Nederland, minervisten, the entirety of Israel, Silicon Valley, Jeff Koons, and Joanne Rowling. The rest of the list is a secret, but if you fw any of these people you can make a fair judgement.

So yeah, on with the show! If you want to contact me because you know about a cool tool or are making a cool tool, feel free! I might develop more issues of this mini-mag if I like making it, so you'll have that to look forward to. Contact info below!

Your Tools Hate You

A manifesto about tools and those who exploit them

The tools of the game-making trade are not neutral. They leave fingerprints all over. Creative software overall has become a gray mass of professionalism.
Over are the days of Kai’s Power Goo and Bryce 3D. We live in the slate-gray omnipresence of Unity, Unreal, and Adobe now, and they hate you.

They hate the crusty, ugly shit you make, they hate the low-bitrate, badly exported, barely looping audio files; the complete absence of SVGs in your workflow; your total lack of programming principles and design patterns; the pervert shit you unavoidably added to get your and strangers’ rocks off; the fact that your placeholder textures are an infringement of about 52 different intellectual properties (including but not limited to Family Guy and Left 4 Dead).

Most of all, you are a horrible person for wanting to enjoy the act of making digitally.

These are PROFESSIONAL tools,

made for PROFESSIONAL people.

You are not a PROFESSIONAL.

you are a HOBBYIST.

Q. What is a HOBBYIST?

A. Scum of the earth, deigns to play and have fun with a big round lollipop and a propeller hat. They can be found conniving on ‘Itch’ (like a rash) and ‘Discord’ (which they sow). Their diseased claws can at times be found wrapped around the tools not meant for them, often unfairly gained through Criminal Activity On The Internet. If possible they would be jailed and killed for this transgression, but they hide behind anonymized codenames like ‘bdgravy977’ or ‘shoebby’.

Q. What is a PROFESSIONAL?

A. Knowers of things, decent folk, obedient, malleable, respectable, and competent in their craft. How they arrived at this point is irrelevant and should be ignored. Their reason for working in games is always pure unadulterated love for the craft, pay is always secondary. Tools are made for them, they don’t need to tell them what they need, the toolmakers can intuit this through an ancient ritual, where a council of bloated flies is summoned to trumpet the needs of the PROFESSIONAL. These are also known as shareholders.

A tool made for professionals is not a toy, it wants to be taken seriously. It models itself for efficiency and drapes itself in an inoffensive-because-it-doesn’t-speak user interface. Some of these tools are abuse victims, pummeled into submission by Market Demands, shedding the quirks and sparks of joy originally made and cherished by its creators and users, until they are Perfect.

A quick eulogy for Clippy:

Clippy, ever since I laid eyes on you I hated you.

I hated how you interrupted me, how you assumed everything about me, how you kept insisting on taking over and doing it your way.

It was like I had a suspiciously fiscally adept little brother, constantly stealing my mouse to make budget sheets while I was trying to make Mario (from Super Mario) in Word using a table.

But you were also the first software feature to make me feel something of this degree. Made me be in dialogue with the MS Office suite. Even if it was often a fight, it remains a dear memory.

Nowadays tools don’t fight with me anymore. They don’t impose weird restrictions or quirks in their design. They are perfect, and in being so are nothing.

I’ll always miss you Clippy, and I fear the day of your LLM resurrection, which by this point feels inevitable.


Department-inflicted Adobe-reliance and Maya-reliance is a tangible thing, harming these students when they exit the cushy student licenses from their school and enter the real world of $70,-/month licenses, €176,-/month licenses, and €441,57/year licenses.

I posit to give up on these tools totally, pretend they do not exist. You do not need to consider paying for them or even pirating them. They are toxic, noxious, and fascistic in nature. Industry standards don’t matter. Your voice and your games matter. I and everyone who’s real wants to play your games, and you don’t need ZBrush for that:

  • ZBrush will make you learn what a ‘pixol’ is.
  • ZBrush will make you start caring about things like ‘retopology’ and ‘polycount’.
  • ZBrush will infect you with the ‘fidelity == quality’ prion disease.
  • ZBrush will ensure that your project will never be finished in your lifetime.
This also applies to Unreal, Unity, and the entire Adobe Creative Suite.

Some tools are free though, the F/OSS guy says, they are nice, he says, they are ethical, he says, but he neglects to mention that they are also deeply insecure.

They want to be taken seriously like the professional tools. The big boys. The bullies who used to make fun of their clunkiness and furry-adjacent mascots. These include tools like Krita, Blender, GIMP, Godot, DaVinci Resolve, Inkscape. Their sin is not as clear-cut; it’s the dejected acceptance of what they are ‘supposed to be’, posited by industries that would never consider to adopt them in the first place.

These tools are the reppers of digital tools. They are ashamed of themselves and what they could be.

Now we find ourselves in the camp of opposition. A ragtag bunch of tools that are not professional tools, and are not self-consciously fitting themselves into a David-Byrnian suit. They are weird and leave weirdly shaped fingerprints all over everything made with them.

Electric Zine Maker, Playscii, Kid Pix, Mario Paint, Aseprite, Dieback, Inspirit, Dither Machine, PICO-8, GB Studio, Narrat, Windowpane, Bapalon, Plano, ANIMEVOX, Decker, Tape Window, (Super) Videotome(:ADV) (Heartbreak), Calico, Pixelbox, Watabou’s Generators, F15H 74NK, Bosca Ceoil, Catch A Poem, vdcrpt, wareware, Magic Wand, Pixel Vision 8, Bfxr, PicoCAD (2), Bitsy, Ren’Py, Textreme (2), and that’s just the ones I’ve used and remember.

People, real people with supposed flesh and blood, are making tools for you and I to use. To make faggot games, to make ragebait, to tell stories in forms that the most annoying guy in the hackerspace will refer to as “not a real game”. And yes, these will get very little attention because they are so uncompromising or unusual in what they do, but that is fine. I think it’s wonderful even, it’s not called a toolbelt for nothing. An artist uses a breadth of tools that are at their disposal, a theoretical tool made to do everything effectively does nothing. Just look at Affinity. These tools say nothing, and you should want your tools to scream at you at all times.

However, ultimately, you should be making and modifying tools yourself. Tools are not made to sit along the sidelines; they should be dynamic, they should respond to your work and your work should respond to them in turn.

Digital art is the only place where it is deemed normal to pay a monthly fee to use a tool you do not own and cannot alter. A painter would laugh in your face. A writer would be dumbstruck. A musician would hum a self-amused tune. Detach your art and self-worth from tools that hate you, and don’t feel self-conscious about doing so. Being a professional is not worth the loss of self.

This Manifesto was written in Deepdwn, 11 hours before a funeral, and sits in solidarity with tiny tools.

Art Toy

/ɑːt tɔɪ/

In my Thesis Cannons, Dynamite Erasers, and Screaming Into The Void I define 'Art Toys' as digital art-making tools that feature playful and exploratory modes of interaction that prioritise the process of making rather than the creation of a final product, and for the purposes of this mini-mag that's still generally correct!

An additional note to make however is how these tool-toys often aim to reimagine the very act of making something digitally. Remaking Maya is one thing, but questioning Maya's method for creating 3D models is something else entirely. ZBrush can be seen as an example of this, even if it has become the standard for sculpting highly detailed models by now.

It being founded by a guy with an alias as silly and 'gamer' as "Pixolator" should tell you something. It fundamentally rethinks 3D modelling as a playful, claylike act, rather than a precise, CAD-like process of extrusion and bevel.

It's so good and fun that it has been widely adopted, bought by Maxon, and turned into a impossibly priced, bloated, subscription-based product!

Anyways, yeah, I don't think it's a good idea to forego digital tools that might not exude 'playfulness' by their design, even if a tool is highly effective at what it does it can still be an art toy!
They might not be super silly or aesthetically 'toy', but I count them as art toys (or alt-tools) because they are open-ended, welcoming, and straight-forward; they make art-making accessible and intuitive, which leads into play! It's a toy not because it looks like one, but because it behaves like one and leads into similar behaviours.

F15H 74NK

You might be mistaken to believe that F15H 74NK is a polygon drawing program, with the various creations you can find in the comments; in actuality it is a music toy!
Tone is affected by a given polygon's shape, size, colour; providing a new dimension to music-making to lead into compelling results! Not to mention the intrinsic appeal of hearing what a poly-drawing of a given videogame character sounds like.

Playscii

Playscii takes the age-old art of ASCII-based drawing and hones it to perfection. Offering a pleasant drawing interface, great color palettes, and intriguing character sets to use; it's as expansive as it is uncompromising.
I've noticed a bunch of outlets and magazines using this sort of grid-based look in their design more often, not to mention Marathon's design language, so strike the iron while it's hot and make some ASCII art!

Squidward Engine

Based on Spongebob's old, web-based Game Builder this fan(?)made iteration proves itself to be a surprisingly solid 2D platforming engine! It's cute and fun to play around with, like just about any functional level editor. Give it a spin!

Ophagirl Dollmaker Template

Ever wanted to make your own dress-up game? Hailing from a long lineage of dollmaker templates by Ninique and Lottev1991, this iteration serves as a solid foundation for your very own. Although it lacks snapping, I find the snap-less way of making to be quite freeing and open-ended! Give it a spin and check out the creator's visual novels while you're at it, they're incredible.

BECOME A GREAT ARTIST IN 10 SECONDS

It may be an incredibly smug piece of software, but its so much fun to play around with! Just by ways of most keys doing something means that you can smash, write words, write in patterns, and the crunchy patterns it creates can be quite pretty and useful for game assets!

OTS-01

A virtual synth with some incredible surprises up its sleeve. Drawing waveforms is a fun and accessible way to dip your toes into sound design, and this is one of the more elegant iterations on this idea. Maybe take a look at the screws as well, they seem a bit loose... hehe,,,

Orca

EVERY LETTER DOES SOMETHING???

In John Conway's Game of Life combinations of pixels are awakened by their neighbours and their relative positions. Constructing a so-called 'automaton' here can lead to a number of outcomes. They can destroy themselves near-instantly, they can do nothing, or, if you're lucky and/or knowledgeable enough, they can move and exhibit faux-intelligent behaviours, sometimes in mesmerizing ways.

Orca, to me, is the auditory equivalent to Conway's critter machine. Developed by Hundredrabbits, as part of the suite of software for their own virtual machine Uxn, it offers a fresh new way of 'programming' music, different even from its contemporaries like Strudel (which is still really cool and worth checking out!!!)

Offline First

To talk about Orca I first need to elucidate a bit on the artist duo responsible for spawning it. Rek and Devine are developers of playful, low-tech, free, and open-source software who position their practice extremely clearly; tools that fully function offline, can be ran on 20 year old hardware without issue, open to be studied, modified, and repaired, anticolonialist, antifascist, and opposed to oppressive and coercive power structures of every kind. They also live on a boat.

Over the years they've shown not only their technical expertise through their tools (albeit impressive to a daunting degree), they also just have incredible ideas about and for dastardly simple software. Taking Orca's sibling Dotgrid as an example, it is a vector drawing program that works off of a grid-and-points system, drawing lines between the points. It's so disarmingly simple and clever, and it's something you'll notice in more of their tools. They might not do a ton, but the things they do they do incredibly well.

Getting Started

Check out the Orca guided tours and tutorials by Allieway Audio! They're old but incredibly helpful if you want to get started.

So you open up Orca and see some text, which disappears into a dark grid the moment you click somewhere. Scary! Not to worry, there's documentation practically everywhere! On the repository, on the website, inside of the zipped folder you just downloaded; and that documentation will say something beautiful: every key on the keyboard is a function. Perhaps even more compellingly, these functions can fit together and interact with one another. One function's output can be another's input in a game of spatial puzzling not too dissimilar from Factorio or Satisfactory.

Check out the VSTs by Unplugred, they're incredible and toy-like and fit perfectly with Orca. My favourites include Magic Carpet and Clickbox!

You build a factory that produces MIDI 'bangs', which you will need to send somewhere for further processing. This can be anything with a MIDI input, like a synth and DAWs like Ableton or, my personal choice, FL Studio. Here the signal can be picked up and processed and turned into actual sound, whether it be a sample or a generator. Here another layer of play is introduced through knobs, dials, VSTs, filters, samplers; all influencing not only the sound but arrangement of your sound-production.

With that all set up you can actually start to write some noise-making creatures. It can be as simple as attaching a Clock to a Track and Delay to send a sequence of notes to the desired channel. In the end it's not difficult to get it making sound, but wrangling the sound into something pleasant is where the challenge lies. Not to mention the grid-based spatial challenges!

A visually pleasing, jumbled mess of notes is easy to arrive at, but with the right sound design even that can be beautiful as ambience or harmonic backing.

Computation is the Point

Orca has no pretenses about what it is and produces, it is deeply computational and embraces it with open arms. In contrast, there are plenty of math and logic functions to further drive this point. There has always been a subsection in digital music production that is avoidant of its own digital nature, which aims to reproduce traditional instrumentation as perfectly as possible. I've always found this a bit of an odd pursuit, albeit an understandable one. It's convenient to not have to organize a full orchestra, but doesn't that miss the point of orchestral scores?

RATED

I find digital's pursuit of new and compelling timbre much more appealing, but the very notation of digital music often remains constrained by a piano roll. People (read: Wendy Carlos) kind of nailed it with modular synthesis, but in a seeming effort to make digital more accessible and more alike to traditional music-making it has become constricted in structures like a child spinning themselves into curtains. FL Studio's Soundgoodizer is an absolute evil.

Tools like Orca take the very composition of music and move it into digital, an inhuman interface to match the artifice of synthesis. Its logic remains scrutible while it presents a new way to think about playing notes; composing with clocks and moving blocks, melding human and machine interface, foregoing constriction, embracing the signal.

Plano

Collaging... The webby way!

I was 11 years old when I found Yume Nikki, a game about dreaming that would wind up shaping me as a game maker and game player. It was the first videogame where the only expectation of me was to explore, I barely knew about the different 'effects' you could collect.

Other exploration games are quick to give you goals, like in Minecraft; fell trees, build a crafting table, make tools, mine ore, and before you know it you're asked to go to hell and kill a dragon. It's exciting, it draws you in, and it's game-y. Yume Nikki, and games like it, instead seem more interested in a less-active form of exploration; take in the sights, interact with the few things that let you, more akin to a museum than a box of Lego's.

I would even dare to say it's anticolonial game design, where by being asked or forced to respect a (game) environment for what it is, it turns our activity into something more thoughtful and observant. Let's take a look at a game engine for making games like these.

Like a Pancake

Flatgames are a loosely defined genre of games where you walk and hear and see from a top-down 2D perspective. Plano is for making these kinds of games and nothing else. I personally adore this all-out commitment of a game engine, be it for visual novels like Renpy or walk-and-talk games like Bitsy. It adds this interesting sort of sheen to a game made in these tools, where the engine is allowed to be a participating presence post-compile.

Kate Bagenzo also created Windowpane, a tool for making web-based hyperlink art, for example visual novels!
It's fun to use and very open-ended, and a good way to learn the basics of HTML and CSS.

In Plano games this can be felt through not only an iconic loading screen, but the way a given character moves, the collage-y use of images as background elements, and the supportive role of text that can be written and placed in a similarly collage-y manner; this contrasts generalistic game engines, who make themselves small after their bootup splash.

So what do these flatgames do? Turns out that even with limitations, and perhaps thanks to them, they prove versatile. Kate Bagenzo, the developer of the tool, has written a manifesto (Sticky Fingers) and recreated a memory (the old view fro the bedroom window) inside of it.

A Plano-powered game that jumped out to me was At least there's lilacs by Sraëka Lillian. As a little blue alligator type thing you look for bits and pieces of poem. The text is fractured and torn apart, spread out over a large area to make the reader meander through and look for the prose. It can take some time, but at least there's lilacs!

A Game Is A Place

Let's circle back on the anticolonial thing, because I think it's worth writing about in relation to Plano and other flat(-ish)games. Where in most exploration games you externalise your exploration in some way (usually by changing or affecting your direct environment) these games instead frame the environment as-is. There are no factories to build, no enemies to wipe out, no resources to harvest; you are an observer and a guest, felt through the very mechanics granted to you.

This very granting of mechanics is what frames your game: if you give players a gun they're going to try and shoot everything. Just like how in Yume Nikki, the moment players find the knife effect they start stabbing anything and everything just to see what it does.

Plano seems to understand this well, and know that in order to have people engage with a collage game the focus needs to be on the background rather than the character. While moving around offers a compelling sense of spatiality, the lack of anything else helps draw focus to what would normally be seen as the background. You walk around not to reach a specific destination either, but to get to see more of the background.

RATED

This kind of game is not without its considerable number of detractors, however. Often considered to "not count" as a game due to the lack of interactivity, the choice to omit is not counted as a design decision but laziness. A favourite of mine is when a lack of jumping in a first-person game is counted as a pure slight, for example in "walking simulators". In The Stanley Parable this is even made into a lengthy gag ending with the jump circle.

I want to argue that game engine options like Plano help develop this perspective further, that games don't have to be defined by the features they lack but rather the features they do have and the reasons why they have them. I think it's good practice to assume intention when engaging with art, more controversially that it's a base-line requirement.

At the end of the day Plano works from a place of 'smallness', where games don't have to be hours-long epics or dopamine-sick retinatainment. Games can be slow, small, and simple. In an age where everything is technically possible, choosing to do little is a radical act.

Wareware

The walkie-talkie builder of my dreams...

I have developed a huge and aggressive love for what I like to call 'walkie-talkies'. These are interactive experiences, typically videogames, where the gameplay primarily consists of walking around and talking to people. Smile For Me always comes up as a great example as it further exemplifies the core tenets of a solid walkie-talkie. It should have a cast of compelling and loveable characters, a solid atmosphere, and an environment worth exploring. Frog Detective also checks these boxes for instance, as does Chants of Sennaar, and I've always wanted to make one of my own.

It's daunting though, what makes the walkie-talkie special is vibes at the end of the day, the vibes have to be -immaculate-. Without vibes you have no desire to walk, or talk, and you'd rather boot up anything else. Sewer Rave is 100% vibes. It does not matter that every wall ceiling and floor is the same brick texture, or that every rat is the same recoloured sprite. The music is bumping, there's a huge snake, and every rat has something funny, endearing, or charming to say. We need more Sewer Rave-type games, and that's why I'm so glad Wareware is in development.

The Walkie

Moving in Wareware feels like walking with toddler legs in the best way possible. It feels clumsy, silly, slippery, especially when there are any slopes involved. It's like fighting against gravity or being sent tumbling down by it. It all feels so incredibly right, not to mention the adorable walking sounds!

The Talkie

NPCs, or 'sprites' in Wareware, are the things and creatures you interact with. Each can be assigned a set of actions, like a message or an event of some sort, even including CSS crimes! Albeit simple, this set of actions is enough to get a rudimentary game loop going, and rings similar to something like Scratch, albeit fully code-less (for now?)

This is a Demo?

It's kind of insane to me that the current, already highly polished state of Wareware is a 'demo', and it has made me excited for things to come. Expanded game logic? An undo button? There's a few things I'm tentatively wishing for, like a basic inventory system so that items can be 'fetched' for NPCs. This enables a game structure where chains of quests can link into eachother, where one NPC might want a pot for their plant but for this pot you need to first help another NPC find a hat for their pet fish but this hat is locked behind a gate and the key is owned by someone who wants dance classes... You see what I mean. It's the interpersonal equivalent of a Rube Goldberg machine, where getting one thing puts the whole machine into motion (preferably with some stutters and hiccups along the way).

Another thing I'm wishing upon the stars for are conditionals! If... Else... For... While... They're probably a pain in the ass to implement but they'd take this engine far into mindfuck territory with how much the possibility space would open up. Areas could be locked off until a certain condition is met, and NPCs could have different actions based on which conditions do or don't apply.

This is me slipping into feature-brain a bit though so take this section with a pinch of salt, if anything it's the prospect of more that is making me dream, and I'm just glad that Wareware will keep evolving further unlike the numerous tiny tools that get uploaded and abandoned by their creator, never to be added to or updated again (VisunovOS I swear I won't do this to you please you have to believe me).

Dreaming of Places

An example of a very Wareware-like game is Magic Wand by thecatamites, might've inspired it? I don't know! But I do know that Procuring estrogen for your toxic slime girlfriend is a stellar example of a Wareware-made game!

Fundamentally the itch that walkie-talkies scratch is a specific one, vaguely in the direction of so-called third places. The way you interact with these digital spaces is strikingly human, you walk and you talk, actions which ideally are done multiple times every day. To locomote yourself is to go someplace and exist there, preferably with others, where you can exchange thoughts, ideas, jokes, and experiences. Walkie-talkies work to simulate this to a certain extent by crafting places that are hospitable, which invite your presence through mechanics and dialogue. As the player you are wanted.

RATED

It's also kind of like a social anxiety power fantasy.

With the recent world cup it has never been clearer that we're running out of third places, running out of space in general to cheer and celebrate to our hearts content. I'm not big on football, but I am big on letting people enjoy things, and the constraints that are put onto human celebration are asphyxiating. You're given one hour to celebrate your team winning a key match, after which you are expected to disperse back into your homes and out of the public eye. People high on emotion lash out when constricted, pressed and pushed. It doesn't feel right, it doesn't feel okay.
When there's no community center to welcome me and offer me space, no park without NIMBYs to call the cops on me for laughing too loud, no bars or venues to spend a night out without paying for a ticket, I start to dream of a sewer full of dancing and drinking rats, eager to talk with me and welcome me into their midst.
